The History of Mosaic Art
Mosaic art, also transliterated as mosaic (English: Mosaic likely derives from "musa," the name of nine Greek goddesses who govern science, poetry, and art), is a two-dimensional artwork also known as mosaic painting, inlay work, or mosaic mural. It is a decorative art form typically using many small stones or colored glass fragments to create patterns. In churches, stained glass is also called stained glass. Today, the term "mosaic" generally refers to this type of colorful visual effect. Mosaics are often used as floor and wall decorations, and were particularly popular in the ancient Roman world. Today, mosaics are not only used for wall and floor decorations but also as works of art, amateur handicrafts, and forms of industry and architecture.

Mosaic has a long history, originating in Mesopotamia around 3000 BC. Gravel mosaics were created by Tiryns in the Mycenaean period of Greece. Mosaics with patterns and images were very popular in the Classical period, both in ancient Greece and Rome. From the 4th century onwards, early Christian cathedrals were decorated with mosaics for walls and ceilings. Mosaic art flourished from the 6th to the 15th centuries. This Byzantine tradition was adopted in the 12th century by the Normandy dynasty of Sicily, the Venetian Republic influenced by the East, and the Rus' people of Ukraine. Mosaic technology fell out of favor during the Renaissance, but artists like Raphael continued to use this ancient technique. Jewish artists, influenced by Rome and Byzantium, decorated the floors of synagogues in the Middle East with mosaics in the 5th and 6th centuries.
In early Islamic art, mosaics with figurative patterns (though mostly without figures) were widely used in religious buildings and palaces, including the Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem, the first great religious building of Islam, and the Great Mosque of Omiya in Damascus. After the 8th century, this type of mosaic fell out of favor in the Islamic world; only geometric patterns, such as the Zelij, remained popular in several regions.

Modern mosaics are made by artists and craftspeople worldwide. Besides traditional stone, ceramic inlays, enamel, and stained glass, many other materials are used, including shells, beads, incantations, chains, gears, coins, and fragments of clothing and jewelry.

The Origin and Development of Taiwanese Mosaic Art
1. Origins and Foundation (Japanese Occupation Period to the 1950s)
Pioneer Yen Shui-long: Revered as the "Father of Taiwanese Mosaic Art," Yen Shui-long was captivated by mosaic art during his studies in Europe and dedicated himself to promoting "the art of everyday life."
Cultural Integration of "Cutting and Pasting": The cutting and pasting technique (collage of broken porcelain pieces) in traditional Minnan architecture was considered a form of "three-dimensional mosaic," providing Yen Shui-long with a local technical foundation for his work.
Concept: Yen Shui-long emphasized that mosaic art should enter public life, enhancing social aesthetics, and not merely be decoration.

2. Development and Prosperity (1960s-1980s)
Public Art Promotion: Yen Shui-long was commissioned to create several large-scale public mosaic murals, such as "Sunflowers" at Taichung Taiyangtang Bakery, "Sports" at TNational Taiwan University of Sport, and "From Agricultural Society to Industrial Society" at  Jiantan Park, Taipei.
Industry Drive: In the 1960s and 70s, Taiwan's ceramic industry (such as Yingge) began producing unglazed mosaics, a durable and low-cost building material, which led to the application of mosaics in exterior walls and floor tiles.
Mosaic Tiles: With the development of industrial society, mosaic mosaics became popular and were used in household sideline production, resulting in colorful tiling patterns that were ubiquitous.


3. Contemporary Evolution (1990s to Present)
Art and Decoration in Parallel: With the development of the times, mosaic art is not only used for exterior building decoration but also combined with digital output technology and innovative materials, resulting in more diverse forms of expression.
"Rectification" and Innovation: Contemporary artists are attempting to move beyond the view of mosaics as mere architectural accessories, striving to elevate them to pure artistic creation and incorporating local Taiwanese elements such as temple and historical site restoration and local art landscaping. Taiwanese mosaic art has undergone a transformation from traditional cut-and-paste techniques to modern tile inlay. Yen Shui-long's localized practices, along with the development of Taiwan's ceramics industry, have jointly shaped Taiwan's unique mosaic cultural heritage.
